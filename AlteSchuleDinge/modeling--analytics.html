Modeling & Analytics
This knowledge area embodies a variety of data driven analytics, geocomputational methods, simulation and model driven approaches designed to study complex spatial-temporal problems, develop insights into characteristics of geospatial data sets, create and test geospatial process models, and construct knowledge of the behavior of geographically-explicit and dynamic processes and their patterns.

Flow Routing and Watershed Analysis

Description
Conceptualize Flow Modeling - The methods for modeling hydrological flow are numerous and nontrivial. They involve cumulative terrain parameters based on flow tracing and routing such as flow-path length, flow accumulation, stream networks, watershed boundaries, and ridge lines, in addition to computing flow direction, and tracing through depressions and flat areas. Becoming familiar with the various geospatial techniques and workflows for performing hydrologic, erosion, and surface water analysis is valuable to the caliber of knowledge and robust skillset required of competent geospatial analysts.​
Analysis Procedures
Flow modeling and routing computation, and watershed analysis methods performed on the given DEM of our study area in ArcPro were simply run from tools directly from the Spatial Analyst Toolbox’s Hydrology set of tools (which are also part of the Arc Hydro Pro Tools toolbox add-in from ESRI, a much more robust and specialized toolbox for hydrology modeling and analysis). Fill (sinks), Flow Direction, Flow Accumulation, Basin, Flow Length, Stream Link, and Watershed comprised the workflow of geoprocessing steps employed on the 10m NED raster map. Derived streams from the process were then compared visually in maps against official Wake County streams (vector feature layer). 
Results
The result of computing flow direction, flow accumulation, and subwatersheds with cell size of 10000 produced four distinct subwatersheds containing the respective computed stream flows. With the Wake County streams vector layer (red lines) overlayed on computed streams, results show a very imperfect, yet approximate output with a lot of room for process improvement. 
Reflection
Hydrology and hydrography rank at the top of my favorites list of major areas of geospatial analysis science. My connection to these particular aspects is on a personal level, it being the case that I have been an avid kayaker of both flatwater and whitewater rivers for many years. Flow, accumulation, and stream delineation operations - especially when deriving from the latest and greatest multispectral aerial imagery - proffer a means by which I can connect even more (and on a scientific versus recreational level) with my environmentalist values.

Geomorphometry I: 
Terrain Modeling and
Spatial & Temporal Terrain Analysis


​Description
The concept of Geomorphometry describes the science of quantitative land-surface analysis (also known as the science of digital terrain modeling and analysis) and it draws upon mathematical, statistical, and image processing techniques to quantify the shape of earth’s topography at various spatial scales (geomorphometry.org).
​
Geomorphometry is utilized in improving the mapping and modeling of:
  • landforms
  • soils
  • vegetation
  • land use
  • natural hazards
  • and other terrain surface information.
The objectives of this Terrain Modeling assignment are to become familiar with various means, methods, techniques, processes, workflows, and tools for working with mathematical and digital terrain models, elevation data, point clouds, multiple return data, triangular irregular networks (TIN), regular grid (raster), contour isolines and meshes, interpolating DSMs, and Lidar point cloud analysis and binning.
Analysis Procedures
The terrain modeling process comprised methods for analyzing bare earth and multiple return lidar data properties by binning, as well as methods for interpolating DEM and DSM.
Results
The first computed Point Density raster of Bare Earth lidar point data at one meter grid cell resolution output six categories of unknown types. The second computed Point Density raster of First Return lidar point data at one meter grid cell resolution output 68 categories of unknown types.
Reflection
From working through this assignment, I have gained an appreciation for beginning the processing of point cloud data by first reviewing the metadata and/or properties and identifying the bare earth/ground surface class of points. From this step, height measurements can be more accurately understood, as the bare earth ground surface serves as the “ground” upon which our terrain model and terrain analyses (slope, aspect, curvature) are generated.

TAGS:  DTM, DSM, TIN, LAS
